How to read papers?

2020-10-22 21:10 / 3 min read

Conclusion from the CS230 course taught by Andrew Ng.

Reading research papers

  • Compile a list of papers. You can find them from medium (a website) or some blog posts. (e.g. Github, arXiv)
  • Skip around them. Find the one you need and exclude those written in a bad way.
  • Decide which you want to pay attention to.

Reading a paper

It's a bad way to read from the first word to the last one. Instead, you should take multiple passes below.

  • Read the title, the abstract and the figures to get a very good sense of what the whole paper talks about. Sometimes you even hardly have to read any of the texts in the paper itself.

  • Read more carefully about the introduction, conclusion, figures and then skim the rest part of the paper. You may skim or even skip the related work in this pass, because you might be unable to understand the related work when you first get into a new area and sometimes the related work part is used by the author to make editors feel good about themselves.

  • Read but skip the math.

  • Read the whole paper but skip parts that don't make sense. With the rapid development of the technology, it is usual that the author did not know what should be important in his paper. So he may also write a lot about something that will not make sense in the future.

Questions to ask yourself after reading a paper

  • what did the author try to accomplish?
  • what were the key elements of the approach?
  • What can you use yourself?
  • What other references do you want to follow?

Sources of papers

  • Twiter (@kiankatan, @Andrew Ng ...)
  • ML subreddit
  • NIPS/ICML/ICLR
  • Friends

Resources in the paper

  • Math
    • Read through it.
    • Rederive from scratch. It will help you totally understand and even generate your own novel algorithms in the future.
  • Code
    • Find and run open-source code.
    • Reimplement it form scratch. It's a sign of your total understanding of the algorithms in the paper.

Longer-term advices

  • Read regularly, often 2 to 3 papers a week. Spaced repetition works better than cramming.
  • Print paper down. Sometimes it's convenient to read in paper.
